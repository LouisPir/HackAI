The HRDataset_Expanded_v2 dataset was designed to analyze factors related to employee turnover and to serve as the foundation for a demonstration of an HR decision-support tool. It enables the estimation of resignation risk, the explanation of its underlying drivers, and the visualization of overall trends across an employee population.

This dataset is built from the combination of two tabular sources, HRDataset_v14.csv and IBM.csv. Their enrichment and merging are performed באמצעות the script dataset/Remplissage.py, which ensures the overall consistency of the final dataset. The goal is to obtain a sufficiently rich and structured dataset to support meaningful HR analysis, while remaining suitable for a demonstration context.

The data is structured and tabular in nature, typical of HR information systems. It includes variables such as employee ID, department, job role, salary, satisfaction and engagement levels, performance, absences, employment status, termination reason, and manager. Together, these variables provide a multifactorial view of attrition dynamics.

The main target variable is Termd, used as a proxy for employee departure. A value of 0 indicates an active employee, while a value of 1 indicates an employee who has left the organization. This variable is central to the scoring logic and risk analysis.

Data preparation relies on several key steps automated by the Remplissage.py script. The script loads both source datasets, aligns their schemas, maps certain fields, generates missing values, and merges the observations into a consolidated file. Examples of transformations include marital status mapping, generation of synthetic employee names, conversion of monthly salaries into annual salaries, assignment of existing managers, and the attribution of certain categories through sampling from existing values.

The dataset also contains sensitive or quasi-sensitive attributes, such as gender, race or ethnicity, marital status, and individual HR-related information. These raise several risks, including discrimination, re-identification, managerial misuse, and breaches of confidentiality. It is therefore essential to implement appropriate safeguards, such as data anonymization, minimization, access restrictions, and clear documentation of permitted uses.

In terms of quality, the dataset has several strengths: it is well-structured for demonstration purposes, includes a variety of HR variables, provides a relevant target variable, and is sufficiently consistent for visualization and simple scoring. However, it also has notable limitations. The data is partly synthetic, some columns have been artificially reconstructed, the merging process is partially random, and its representativeness of real-world HR contexts is limited. Additionally, overall quality depends heavily on the mapping choices between the two original sources.

As a result, the use of this dataset must be strictly controlled. It is suitable for academic demonstrations, exploration of HR patterns, data visualization, decision-support prototyping, and experimentation in explainability and fairness. However, it should not be used for real HR decision-making, employee evaluation, disciplinary actions, or production deployment without thorough legal and ethical review.

Finally, several improvements can be considered for a future version. These include better documentation of the final schema, clear separation between generated and original columns, analysis of distributions before and after merging, auditing of sensitive variables, production of a data quality report, and explicit separation of training, testing, and demonstration datasets.
